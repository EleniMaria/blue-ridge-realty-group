How to Prepare Your Home for Sale

Renata Cole April 30, 2026 6 min read

Sellers often ask us the same question first: "What should I fix before listing?" The honest answer is that not everything is worth fixing — and knowing the difference can save you both money and time. Here's how we approach it with our own clients.

Fix this

  • Anything broken or visibly damaged — leaky faucets, cracked tiles, doors that don't close right. These read as neglect, even if the issue is minor.
  • Curb appeal basics — fresh mulch, trimmed shrubs, a clean front door. First impressions happen before a buyer steps inside.
  • Outdated lighting fixtures, which are inexpensive to swap but disproportionately affect how a room feels.

Skip this

  • Full kitchen or bathroom remodels. Buyers rarely pay back the full cost of a major renovation, and tastes vary too much to guess right.
  • Major landscaping overhauls beyond basic tidiness — most buyers will change it anyway.
  • Trendy paint colors chosen to impress rather than appeal broadly. Neutral, well-maintained always outperforms bold and personal.

Staging that actually works

Staging isn't about making a home look like a magazine spread — it's about helping a buyer imagine living there. That usually means decluttering more than decorating: clearing counters, removing excess furniture so rooms feel larger, and depersonalizing just enough that buyers can picture their own life in the space rather than yours.

"The goal of staging is invisibility — if a buyer notices the staging, it didn't work."

Photography matters more than people think

In a market where most buyers see a listing online before they ever see it in person, professional photography isn't a luxury — it's often the difference between a showing and a scroll-past. We always recommend completing prep work before, not after, photos are taken.

If you're not sure where to start, a walkthrough with your agent before listing is the cheapest, most effective tool available — we'll tell you honestly what's worth your time and what isn't, based on what's actually moving in your specific market.
